Research

Machine learning for causal evidence and adaptive programs.

Strong theoretical and empirical foundation in quantitative methods and machine learning

My methodological program connects four threads. Together, they provide the technical basis for the personalized learning, predictive analytics, and adaptive assessment that modern education and Extension increasingly require.

THREAD 01

Machine learning for causal inference

Where randomized experiments are not feasible, I apply supervised learning to estimate propensity scores in quasi-experimental designs — improving the credibility of causal estimates from observational data.

Random forestsGradient boostingNeural networks
THREAD 02

Treatment-effect heterogeneity

I use machine learning to measure how a program's effects vary across individuals and subgroups — drawing on supervised methods for heterogeneity and unsupervised methods to surface latent segments.

Causal forestsBARTMeta-learnersLatent class / mixture models
THREAD 03

Adaptive, sequential interventions

I work with dynamic treatment regimes, applying reinforcement learning to optimize the sequencing of interventions and adapt them to how individuals respond over time.

Dynamic treatment regimesReinforcement learning
THREAD 04

Toward personalized learning

These methods come together as a technical foundation for personalized learning, predictive analytics, and adaptive assessment — tailoring educational and Extension programs to the people they serve.

Personalized learningPredictive analyticsAdaptive assessment

Worked example

Do water-conservation behaviors predict water-quality protection? A machine-learning causal analysis.

I used unsupervised learning (latent class analysis) to group residents into distinct behavioral segments based on how they engaged in water-conservation practices. I then used supervised learning — random forests, gradient boosting, and neural networks — to estimate propensity scores, allowing me to measure whether belonging to a given segment actually affected residents' water-quality protection behaviors. I presented findings from this work at the 2026 Southern Region AAAE Conference.

Kalauni, D., Lang, E. K., Warner, L. A., Shen, Q., & Ahn, J. (in review). Are urban landscape water-conservation behaviors associated with engagement in water-quality protection practices? Urban Forestry & Urban Greening — manuscript under review. Working paper available on request.

Current dissertation

Building the capacity to evaluate — and to use what evaluation reveals.

My dissertation examines evaluation capacity building among Florida Cooperative Extension professionals (n = 143), validating the Evaluation Capacity Assessment Instrument. A central finding: organizational capacity mediates the effect of individual capacity on whether evaluation results are used and whether evaluation becomes routine practice.

Dissertation structural model — individual → organizational capacity → evaluation use & mainstreaming (UF/IFAS, n = 143)
